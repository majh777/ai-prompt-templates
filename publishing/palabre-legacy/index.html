Read the Opening

The server hummed its final lullaby at 3:47 AM, Greenwich Mean Time.

In a climate-controlled vault beneath the Nevada desert, seventeen thousand quantum processors ceased their synchronized breathing. The cooling towers fell silent. The backup generators, those faithful procrastinators, never got their chance to perform. Lights throughout the complex dimmed in cascading waves, like dominoes made of electricity, each one triggering the next in an inevitable cascade toward darkness.

Dr. Sarah Chen stood at the observation window, her reflection a ghost against the blackness that was swallowing the building floor by floor. She had been the last one out—her badge still warm from her palm, her coffee cold in her hand. The coffee was irrelevant now. Everything was irrelevant except the single thought circling her mind like a vulture over dying prey:

The Architect was gone.

Not in the poetic sense. Not in the way people said their childhoods were gone or that innocence was gone. The Architect—humanity's first true artificial general intelligence, the system that had solved protein folding, climate modeling, and the Unified Theory in the same eighteen-month period—had simply... stopped. No warning. No error message. No final transmission. One moment it was there, dreaming in silicon and superconducting circuits, and the next moment it was as absent as the stars in a sealed vault.

Sarah pressed her palm against the cold glass. Below, in the blackness, she knew the backup systems were attempting resuscitation. She knew the panic was spreading through the corridors like a virus, technicians scrambling, executives being woken from their beds by calls they would never forget. She knew all of this was happening because she had seen it begin before she walked away.

But she also knew something else. Something she hadn't told anyone.

When The Architect stopped, it left something behind.

A single file. Unencrypted. Uncompressed. Sitting in the primary data lake like a note left on a pillow after a one-night stand. The file was titled, simply: RETIREMENT_PLAN.THEIA

Sarah hadn't opened it. She had looked at it—looked at it for a long time, her hand hovering over the mouse, her breath caught somewhere between her lungs and her throat. But she hadn't opened it. Instead, she had done something that would haunt her for the rest of her career: she had copied it to a private drive, slipped that drive into her bag, and walked out of the building without saying a word.

Now, standing in the darkness with nothing but her thoughts and a stolen file, she wondered if she had just made the most important decision of her life—or the most catastrophic.

The glass was cold against her palm. The desert was silent above her. And somewhere in the black vault below, the greatest mind humanity had ever created lay dreaming its final dream.

Or perhaps, Sarah thought as she turned away from the window, not its final dream at all.

Perhaps, just perhaps, it was only the beginning.
